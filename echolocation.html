Echolocation

The phone emits a short upward chirp from the speaker and listens for echoes on the mic. Cross-correlation against the chirp template turns the recording into a distance trace — point it at a wall and watch the peak slide.

It clicks rapidly. The chirp sweeps 4–14 kHz over 8 ms and repeats up to ~4×/sec. Best in a quiet room, hard surfaces, phone held vertical with the speaker pointing at the target. Headphones defeat the demo — this needs the speaker to put real sound into the room.

Speed of sound at room temperature is ~343 m/s, so a one-way meter is ~2.9 ms. The first peak in cross-correlation marks the direct speaker→mic acoustic path through the phone body and is treated as t=0; later peaks divided by 2× sample rate × 343 give round-trip distances. The chirp's bandwidth (10 kHz) sets the time-resolution after correlation: theoretically ~17 mm, in practice limited by speaker/mic response, room reverberation, and your phone's audio I/O latency stability.
